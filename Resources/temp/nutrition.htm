Calorie

    The amount of energy needed to raise the temperature of a kilogram of water 1 degree Celsius.  Calories are units of energy and cannot be created or destroyed, only converted from one form to another (i.e. from fat to motion). 

Carbs have 4 calories per gram

Protein has 4 calories per gram

Fat had 9 calories per gram.  This is why your body stores excess energy in fat.

Your body is able to convert the energy from fat into protein and carbs, and visa versa.  So it does not matter what form of calorie you ingest.  There are some irregularities on the efficiency of absorption, but it is usually a small difference and so it is not wise to rely on this to lose weight.

BMR

    The BMR is your bodies base metabolic rate.  We have included two forms of metabolism into this number.  The first is the classical BMR which is calculated by how much you weight, with a small modification due to the amount that is muscle.  The second component is the number of calories burned that you do not account for in the activity journal.  If you are good at recording your activities the number should agree well with the number that most calculators will give.  This number determines the number of calories you need to eat to maintain your weight.

Fat

    Fat is the bodies primary storage medium for calories.  It consists of a chain of carbon atoms with an acid at the end.  Fat has gotten a bad rap from well meaning nutritionist who were working with bad data.  Fat also was a villain for the weight loss group as it has more calories per gram. 

    The first thing you should know is that not all fat is bad for you, or your waistline.  Fat makes food taste better, helps with hunger and serves essential functions in your body.  Fat is essential for the absorption of  vitamins A, D, E, and K, and carotenoids  A moderate fat diet has been shown to be healthier for your heart than low fat diets.  However, not all fat has been created equally.  The reason there was confusion before was that experiments had not been done to measure the effect of the various types of fat.

    Fat should be eating in moderation from sources that have not had fat added.

Trans Fat

    Trans fats are the baddest of the bad.  They are made when oils are made into solid fats by an artificial process.  They do occur naturally in animal tissues, but it is only a small amount.  Trans fats have been shown to be bad for the heart, cause cholesterol problems, and even to cause weight gain on the same amount of calories as is maintained with other fats.

    Can you completely remove trans fats from your diet? No, as was said before, they do occur naturally, however you can avoid large doses of that fats.

Here are foods that contain large amounts of trans fats (they are now being listed on the labels)

Fast Foods, vegetable shortenings, some margarines, crackers, candies, cookies, snack foods, fried foods, baked goods, and other processed foods.

Saturated Fat

   Saturated fat is the evil cousin of the fat group.  It is not as bad as Trans fat but is still hard on your heart.  You should try to limit the amount of saturated fat you are getting.

These are sources of saturated fat.

  • High-fat dairy products such as full-fat cheese, cream, ice cream, whole milk, 2% milk and sour cream.
  • High-fat meats like regular ground beef, bologna, hot dogs, sausage, bacon and spareribs
  • Lard
  • Butter
  • Fatback and salt pork
  • Cream sauces
  • Gravy made with meat drippings
  • Chocolate
  • Palm oil and palm kernel oil
  • Coconut and coconut oil
  • Poultry (chicken and turkey) skin

 

Monounsaturated Fat

    Monounsaturated fats are considered good fats.  They provide protection for your heart.  These are found primarily the plant sources and can be gained from

  • Avocados
  • Canola oil
  • Olive oil
  • Peanut oil and other nuts
  • Sesame oil


Polyunsaturated Fat

    Polyunsaturated fat is another form of good fats.  It is more likely to become rancid, but it is better for people who have insulin resistance. Omega-3 and Omega-6 fats belong to this group and have shown great health benefits.

  • Grain products
  • Fish and sea food (herring, salmon, mackerel, halibut)

  • Fish oil

Sodium

    Sodium is half of the chemicals that make up salt.  It is essential to the function of the body.  It allows your body to get water into cells, keeps your body's pH correct and helps your nervous system.  Too little sodium and you will die as sometimes happens to endurance athletes.  Too much sodium and your body will experience high blood pressure, edema (swelling)) and other health conditions.  Most Americans get twice the upper recommended limit of sodium.

Sources

  • Fast Food
  • Processed foods
  • Table Salt
  • Canned Foods

Total Carbohydrate

    Carbohydrates are your bodies preferred source of energy.  This is a group of nutrients that varies from fiber to sugar.  Like fats, carbohydrates have gone through a image overhaul in the last few years.  Years ago carbohydrates were the solution to everything, now a more complete picture is emerging that carbohydrates are dangerous and good for you like fats.

    A carbohydrate is made from sugar molecules.  Sugars have only one unit while starches and fiber can have large numbers of sugar units strung together.  In my research I work with sugars strung together by bacteria into molecular rings.  Your digestive system treats all carbohydrates the same, it breaks them into sugar and then converts them to glucose.  How quickly the body is able to break the carbohydrates down is its glycemic index.  This is a measure of how much of a blood sugar spike you will get from a food.  Foods that have a low glycemic index are the best to eat, while high glycemic indexes have been associated with obesity, diabetes, and heart disease.

    Try to get as much of your carbs from whole grains, unprocessed foods and not "white".  These all have been shown to make dieting successful.

Dietary Fiber

  • Whole-grain breakfast cereals

Sugars

    Sugars are the simplest of the carbs.  They are rapidly absorbed and so have high glycemic indexes.  However, they are not bad, only should be eaten in moderation for a healthy lifestyle.  Added sugars should be avoided, but naturally sweet foods are usually a great source of flavor in a diet.

Protein

    Protein is the building block of life.  It is build from amino acids.  Little is known about the amounts of protein needed for optimal health.  There are two limits that are know.  The human body needs around 9 grams of protein for every 20 pounds of body weight to keep tissue from starting to break down.  At the upper limits, too much protein will leech calcium from bones leading to osteoporosis.

Vitamin A

    Vitamin A is a major player in your health.  It supports vision, bone growth, reproduction, cell division, cell differentiation,  regulation of the immune system, which helps prevent or fight off infections.  Vitamin A promotes healthy surface linings of the eyes and the respiratory, urinary, and intestinal tracts.

Vitamin A comes in many different forms, but the body absorbs animal sources most readily.  Here are a list of sources of vitamin A.

  • Milk, margarine
  • Eggs
  • Beef liver
  • Fortified breakfast cereals
  • Carrots
  • Cantaloupes
  • Sweet potatoes
  • Spinach

Vitamin C

    Vitamin C is a protective compound.  It protects your body from the dangers of being alive in a toxic world.  It also is one of the main components of collagen.  Collagen is the main connective tissue of the body.  Without collagen, your teeth fallout, muscles waste, wounds will not heal and scurvy develops.  Sever vitamin C decency is rare, but the more vitamin C you get the better your health will be.  Vitamin C is easily destroyed, so eat a little raw foods to maximize the benefit.

  • Citrus fruits or citrus juices
  • Berries
  • Peppers
  • Tomatoes, broccoli, and spinach
  • Potatoes

Calcium

    Calcium is the most abundant metal in our bodies.  It is the key component of bones, and plays an important role in calming the nervous system.  Although calcium is the most abundant mineral in our body, it is often inadequately supplied by our diets.

Foods that have calcium

  • Tofu, almonds,  peanuts, peas, black beans, and most soy drinks
  • Dairy Products (Milk, cheese, yoghurt...)
  • Broccoli, kale, spinach, tomatoes, Greens
  • Wheat Flour, Cornmeal
  • Sardines, Canned Salmon

Iron

    Iron is important for the energy of your body.  It carries oxygen through the blood.  It is especially important for women to get enough iron.  To little iron and you will be anemic, which makes you tired and weak. The USDA says that over 3/4 of American women had iron intakes below 80 percent of their RDA

    Iron is lost under most cooking conditions.  Cook your food for as little time as possible, with as little water as possible.  Consider using cast iron cookware.

Foods that have iron:

  • Beef, Liver, and Giblets, oysters
  • Enriched whole wheat flour
  • Enriched Rice
  • Tomatoes, watermelon, raisins, and grapefruit, oranges, avocados, strawberries
  • Spinach, tofu, kidney beans
  • Baking Chocolate

    Coffee, tea, milk and EDTA (a chemical additive common in foods) all interfere with iron absorption, so avoid these foods when you are trying to get iron.

    Vitamin C promotes iron absorption, so get a drink of pulpy orange juice with your meal.

Inadequate magnesium intake frequently causes muscle spasms, and has been associated with cardiovascular disease, diabetes, high blood pressure, anxiety disorders and osteoporosis.

Sources of Magnesium

Zinc

    Zinc is an important helper mineral in the body.  It helps regulate a huge number of processes in the body.  Deficiency is rare in the US population, however it is important for alcoholics, elderly, low-income children and vegetarians.

    Symptoms of deficiency include anemia, delayed growth, birth defects, spontaneous abortion, impaired sexual maturation, sterility, delayed wound healing, glucose intolerance, poor appetite and skeletal abnormalities.

Foods with Zinc

Thiamin

    Thiamin was one of the first vitamins discovered.  It is essential to converting your food to energy.  Symptoms of deficiency are difficulty in concentration, irritability, depression, muscle weakness and poor coordination, mental confusion, peripheral neuropathy and edema.  Most food in the US has been enriched to have high levels of thiamin.

Sources

  • Whole grains
  • Nuts
  • Fish, pork
  • Beans
  • Enriched grain products

Riboflavin

    An essential component for many cellular functions.  Decency has been linked to scaly, dry skin, angular stomatitis (cracks in the skin at the corners of the mouth), cheilosis (fissuring of the lips), swollen purplish tongue, photophobia, and burning, itching eyes.

Sources

  • Milk
  • Eggs
  • Enriched cereals and grains
  • Ice cream
  • Liver
  • some lean meats
  • Green vegetables such as broccoli

Niacin

    Niacin is an essential vitamin that supports energy metabolism.  Symptoms of decency are fatigue, poor appetite, diarrhea, irritability, headache, emotional instability and possible memory loss.

Sources

  • Liver
  • Meat
  • Peanuts and other nuts
  • Whole grains

Pantothenic acid

    Pantothenic acid is a key component of the enzymes that break down food.  Decency results in headache, fatigue, listlessness, weakness, personality changes, sleep disturbances, impaired motor coordination, and gastrointestinal symptoms.

Pantothenic acid decency is extremely rare as it is in almost all foods.

Vitamin B6

    Vitamin B6 is essential for the processing of protein.  Decencies cause skin disorders, neurological changes include weakness, tingling and pain in the extremities, as well as depression, headaches and nausea.  This nutrient is easily destroyed by cooking, milling, and processing.  It is also destroyed by flash freezing.  Raw foods and meats are the best sources of this nutrient.

Sources

  • Meat (Fish, poultry)
  • Nuts
  • Fruits
  • Whole grains such as oatmeal

Folate

    Folate is used by the body to correctly build DNA.  It is also essential in the formation of an embryo.  The nutrient is extremely easy to destroy by processing and storage. 

Sources

  • Liver
  • Legumes
  • Nuts

Vitamin B12

    Vitamin B12 makes folate work.  It's other function is to support the nervous system.  Vitamin B12 deficiency will lead to permanent neurological damage.  The vitamin is stable, but is difficult to absorb.  Prolonged cooking will destroy B12.

Sources

  • Meat
  • Some Dairy

Vitamin E

Sources
